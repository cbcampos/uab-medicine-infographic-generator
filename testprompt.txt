Participants  With  and Without  Diabetes

H eart  failure  (HF),  a significant  complication
of diabetes,  develops  in 22%  of cases  and
contributes  to morbidity  and mortality
even  in the absence  of underlying  coronary  artery
disease  (CAD).  1 The  AHA’s  (American  Heart  Associa-
tion)  LE8  (Life’s  Essential  8) framework  measures
cardiovascular  health  (CVH)  using  blood  pressure,
blood  glucose,  blood  lipids,  weight,  physical  activity,
adherence  to a Mediterranean  diet,  nicotine  expo-
sure,  and sleep  health.  2 Optimal  CVH  decreases  the
risk of cardiovascular  disease  in the general  popula-
tion,  2-4 but it is unclear  whether  better  CVH  can
mitigate  the risk of developing  HF among  adults
with  diabetes  or prediabetes.  To address  this  gap,
we examined  associations  between  baseline  CVH
and incident  HF among  adults  with  diabetes,
prediabetes,  and without  diabetes.
METHODS
Baseline  LE8  scores  (range:  0-100)  were  calculated
for HF-free  adults  from  the REGARDS  (REasons  for
Geographic  and Racial  Differences  in Stroke)  study,  a
prospective  ongoing  biracial  cohort  of U.S.
community-dwelling  adults  aged  $45 years,
recruited  from  2003  to 2007  from  all 48 continental
states  with  oversampling  from  the southeastern
United  States.  5 Baseline  data  on sociodemographics
and LE8  behaviors  (physical  activity,  nicotineexposure,  and sleep  health)  were  collected  by phone.
Other  LE8  factors  and behaviors  including  fasting
blood  and urine  samples,  electrocardiograms,  blood
pressure,  anthropometric  measures  (body  mass  index
[BMI]),  diet,  and medication  use were  obtained  dur-
ing an in-home  visit.  REGARDS  study  procedures
were  approved  by the institutional  review  board,  and
all participants  provided  written  informed  consent.
Baseline  diabetes  was  ascertained  based  on fasting
serum  glucose,  random  serum  glucose,  glycosylated
hemoglobin  A 1c , use of antidiabetic  medication,
and/or  self-report,  using  standard  definitions.  The
4 primary  outcomes  included  a composite  measure  of
incident  HF hospitalization  or HF death,  incident  HF
hospitalization  alone,  incident  heart  failure  with
preserved  ejection  fraction  (HFpEF)  (ejection  frac-
tion  [EF]  $50%)  hospitalization,  and incident  heart
failure  with  reduced  ejection  fraction  (HFrEF)
(EF D50%)  hospitalization.  These  were  clinician -
adjudicated  prospectively  between  2004  and 2020
by reviewing  records  for clinical  signs  and symptoms
of HF, B-type  natriuretic  peptide  levels,  and cardiac
imaging  data.  Baseline  CAD  and adjudicated  interim
nonfatal  myocardial  infarction,  occurring  after  the
baseline  and before  the incident  HF hospitalization,
were  included  as covariates.
We excluded  participants  with  baseline  HF
(n  3,948),  who  were  missing  to follow-up  (n  512),
or who  were  missing  data  on sleep  health  (n  7,750)

and satisfied  in fully  adjusted  models  by assessing  an

SAS  version  9.4 (SAS  Institute)  and STATA  version  18
(StataCorp).
RESULTS
In the final  sample  of 17,265  individuals,  6,337
(36.7%)  were  Black,  9,550  (55.3%)  were  women,  3,403
(19.7%)  had diabetes,  3,969  (23.0%)  had prediabetes,
and 9,893  (57.3%)  had neither  diabetes  nor predia-
betes  at baseline.
The  median  baseline  cumulative  LE8  score  for the
overall  sample  was  63 (Q1-Q3:  54-73).  Among  those
with  diabetes,  the baseline  cumulative  LE8  score  was
lower  than  the scores  among  those  with  prediabetes
or no diabetes:  53 (Q1-Q3:  45-61)  compared  with
59 (Q1-Q3:  50-68)  and 69 (Q1-Q3:  59-77),  respectively
(Figure  1A). Participants  with  diabetes  had lower
median  baseline  scores  in the BMI,  blood  pressure,
and glucose  domains.  Only  21%  of participants  with
diabetes  achieved  an “ideal”  score  of 100 in physical
activity,  15%  in lipids,  10%  in BMI,  9% in blood
pressure,  and 7% in diet.
Over  a median  follow-up  period  of 15.8  years  (Q1-
Q3: 9.6-15.8  years),  there  were  986 incident  HF hos-
pitalizations  or HF deaths,  including  356 among
those  with  diabetes.  In multivariable-adjusted  Coxproportional  hazards  models,  a 10-point  higher  LE8
score  was  associated  with  28%  lower  risk of incident
HF events  among  those  with  diabetes  (HR:  0.72;
95%  CI: 0.66-0.79)  (Figure  1B). This  association  was
similar  among  those  without  diabetes.  The  results
were  similar  for HFpEF  and HFrEF  hospitalizations.
The  association  of 10-point  higher  LE8  score  and
incident  HF events  was  also  similar  when  the sample
was  restricted  to individuals  with  diabetes  treated
with  insulin  (HR:  0.74;  95%  CI: 0.60-0.80).  The  as-
sociations  between  LE8  and HF outcomes  were
weaker  among  those  with  prediabetes.
DISCUSSION
This  study  presents  strong  observational  evidence
that  maintaining  better  CVH  even  in the presence  of
diabetes  and regardless  of whether  participants  with
diabetes  were  treated  with  insulin  may  be important
for reducing  risk of HF, including  both  major  sub-
types  of HF, HFpEF  and HFrEF.
Our findings  can offer  clinical  implications  that
might  be important  for developing  future  strategies
to reduce  incident  HF in diabetes.  CVH  metrics  might
provide  a framework  for the development  and
implementation  of interventions  to prevent  HF
among  patients  with  diabetes,  as our study  found
that  a 10-point  higher  LE8  score  was  associated  with
a 28%  lower  risk of incident  HF. This  is important  for
people  with  diabetes  because  diabetes  itself  puts  in-
dividuals  at risk of HF. Our results  underscore  the
importance  of tighter  blood  pressure  and lipids  con-
trol,  physical  activity,  and other  CVH  components  to
prevent  HF even  in the setting  of hyperglycemic
state.
STUDY STRENGTHS  AND  LIMITATIONS.  Our study’s
strengths  include  large  diverse  sample  and rigorously
adjudicated  outcomes  as well  as quality-controlled
data  collection.  Limitations  include  an observa-
tional  design  that  limits  causal  inferences.  The  LE8
score  was  calculated  using  baseline  measures,  and we
were  not able  to control  for the changes  in LE8
metrics  or diabetes  status  over  time.  Some  health
behaviors  were  self-reported  with  known  limitations.
CONCLUSIONS
In this  study  a 10-point  higher  LE8  score  was  asso-
ciated  with  a 28%  lower  risk of incident  HF among
those  with  diabetes.  Maintaining  CVH  may  mitigate
the risk of de novo  HF in diabetes  despite  the risk
